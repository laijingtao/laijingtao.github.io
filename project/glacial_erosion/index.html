Spatial patterns of glacial erosion in mountains

 

Climate plays a significant role in controlling the evolution of alpine landscapes by influencing the rate and pattern of alpine glacier erosion. The equilibrium line altitude (ELA) is a common way to collapse climate into a single variable. The ELA is an integration of various factors controlling glacial mass balance and the position of the ELA is largely controlled by local temperature and snowfall rates. The location of the most rapid erosion is suggested to coincide with the intersection of the ELA and topography because the efficiency of glacial erosion is often assumed to be highest where the ice flux is at the maximum. However, theoretical models of glacial erosion indicate that the rate of glacial erosion is closely related to the basal sliding velocity and/or the subglacial water pressure fluctuation, rather than ice flux. Warm-based ice is therefore expected to have greater potential for erosion than cold-based ice. The different erosive power of warm-based versus cold-based ice has been suggested to be responsible for the formation of dissected low-relief plateaus on many glaciated continental margins. In a polythermal ice sheet, warm-based ice streams have been operating selective linear erosion of deep valleys and fjords, and the frozen-bed patches between ice streams have caused limited glacial modification.

The effect of basal thermal regime, however, has not been well explored for alpine glaciers. Previous analytical and numerical models either assume the glacier is all warm-based or only include a very simple calculation for basal temperature. Such treatment is practical because it reduces the complexity of the model, and it is also reasonable because most modern alpine glaciers in low- and mid-latitude regions are temperate and polythermal alpine glaciers only exist in high-latitude regions such as Canadian Arctic and high-altitude areas such as the European Alps. During glacial periods, the climate was cooler than today and polythermal glaciers are expected to be more common. For example, southern Patagonian Andes, where mountains have remained high elevations above the ELA of the Last Glacial Maximum and the bedrock cooling ages are old, may represent an example of glacial protection of an active orogen from erosion through an increase of cold-based conditions.

We explore how the spatial pattern of glacial erosion varies in different climates as a result of different basal thermal structures by using numerical modeling. We have built a glacial erosion model using the Parallel Ice Sheet Model (PISM). Given bedrock topography, annual average near-surface air temperature at sea level, and annual precipitation rates, PISM computes the evolution of the thermal and dynamic states of the glacier over time. We have implemented a simple erosion model into PISM. The rate of erosion is modeled as a linear function of the basal sliding velocity.

I presented some results of this study at the GSA 2018 Annual Meeting. If you want to learn more about this research, contact me.
