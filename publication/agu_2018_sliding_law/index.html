A comparison of basal sliding and erosion in numerical glacial landscape evolution models using two different sliding laws

Jingtao Lai, Alison M. Anders
December 2018
Project

Abstract

Alpine glaciers have significantly impacted landscapes of many mountain ranges. Theoretical models link the rate of erosion by abrasion and quarrying to the rate of basal sliding, and numerical landscape evolution models typically assume that the erosion rate is proportional to the sliding velocity raised to some power. A careful treatment of basal sliding is therefore essential to model glacial erosion. The shallow ice approximation (SIA) scheme has been used to model ice flow in many numerical landscape evolution models, and these models rely on an empirical sliding law that relates sliding velocity to ice surface gradient. However, this sliding law lacks proper treatment of the stress balance of ice and it may produce unrealistic topography in glacial landscape evolution models. Furthermore, most previous models neglect the conservation of energy or use simplified models to solve the subglacial thermal state. The Parallel Ice Sheet Model (PISM) uses a “hybrid” scheme that combines the shallow shelf approximation (SSA) with the non-sliding SIA. Basal sliding in PISM is modeled by the SSA stress balance and a pseudo-plastic till model for basal resistance, and PISM conserves energy. We conduct a series of numerical experiments using both the traditional SIA scheme and the hybrid scheme to model the glaciation and glacial erosion over a synthetic glacial landscape. Our results show a substantial difference in the spatial and temporal distribution of sliding velocity predicted by the two schemes. The traditional SIA method predicts more temporal variation of sliding rate than the hybrid scheme. The transition from non-sliding ice to sliding ice happens at higher elevation in the SIA scheme than the hybrid scheme. The hybrid scheme predicts realistic glacial landforms such as overdeepening of glacial valleys. Models using the traditional SIA method are prone to numerical instability. Our results demonstrate that using a sophisticated ice dynamics model increases the ability to simulate glacial landscape evolution.
